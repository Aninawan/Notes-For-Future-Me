Aggregate Supply

1. Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

SRAS shows the relationship between price level and output in the short run when wages and expectations adjust slowly.

  • Upward sloping
  • Higher output → higher inflation
  • Linked to labor market conditions

2. Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve describes the inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation.

Lower unemployment → higher wage growth → higher inflation Higher unemployment → lower wage growth → lower inflation

3. Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve

Inflation depends on expected inflation and unemployment gap.

π = πe − σ(u − uf)

  • π = inflation
  • πe = expected inflation
  • u = unemployment rate
  • uf = natural rate of unemployment
  • σ = sensitivity parameter

4. Natural Rate of Unemployment (uf)

When unemployment equals its natural rate:

  • Inflation equals expected inflation
  • No acceleration of inflation

5. Wage-Setting and Price-Setting

  • Wages depend on expected prices and labor market tightness
  • Firms set prices based on wage costs and markup

6. Output Gap and Inflation

Inflation is linked to the output gap:

(Y − Yf) / Yf = −ψ(u − uf)

When output exceeds potential → inflation rises When output below potential → inflation falls

7. Shifts in AS

  • Changes in expected inflation
  • Changes in wage demands
  • Supply shocks (oil prices, productivity)

8. Long-Run Implication

In the long run, unemployment returns to its natural rate and inflation equals expected inflation.

No long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment.
