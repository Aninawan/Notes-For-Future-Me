Consumer Choice and Demand — Lesson Summary & Practice Quiz

01

The Individual Demand Curve

As the price of x falls, the consumer's budget set expands and they move to higher indifference curves — purchasing more x. Tracing these quantity choices against price generates the individual demand curve.

Movement Along the Curve

Caused by a change in the own price p_x. Results in a change in quantity demanded (along the same curve).

Shift of the Curve

Caused by changes in income, prices of other goods (p_y), or tastes. The entire demand curve moves inward or outward.

02

Market Demand

The market demand curve is the horizontal summation of all individual demand curves — for each price, add up the quantities demanded by every consumer.

Two consumers A and B, two goods x and y X = xA + xB = xA(px, py, IA) + xB(px, py, IB) Market demand function X = X(px, py, IA, IB) Example: xA = 10 − 2px − 0.1py + 0.5IA  |  xB = 17 − 0.05px − 0.5py + ... X = 27 − 3px − 0.1py + 0.05(IA+IB)
Key assumption: All consumers are price-takers — each is too small to influence the market price. Everyone faces the same prices p_x and p_y.
03

Own-Price Elasticity of Demand

Measures the proportionate change in quantity demanded in response to a 1% change in the own price.

Own-price elasticity formula εx = (∂x/∂px) · (px/x)  =  %ΔQ / %ΔP Always negative (normal/inferior goods) — convention: use |ε| ε = (∂Q/∂P) · (P/Q)
|ε| < 1
Inelastic
|ε| = 1
Unit Elastic
|ε| > 1
Elastic
Example (U=xy): x* = I/2p_x → ε_x = (−I/2p_x²)·(p_x / (I/2p_x)) = −1. Cobb-Douglas utility always yields unit-elastic own-price demand.
04

Price Elasticity & Total Spending

Total spending = p_x · x. Differentiating with respect to p_x: ∂(p_x·x)/∂p_x = x[1 + ε_x]

Elasticity Type|ε| ConditionPrice ↑Price ↓
Inelastic|ε| < 1Spending ↑Spending ↓
Unit Elastic|ε| = 1No changeNo change
Elastic|ε| > 1Spending ↓Spending ↑
Intuition: If demand is inelastic, the quantity fall from a price rise is proportionally smaller than the price rise → total revenue increases. If elastic, quantity falls more than price rises → revenue falls.
05

Income & Cross-Price Elasticities

💰

Own-Price Elasticity

ε_x = (∂x/∂p_x)·(p_x/x)

Normal/inferior: always negative. Giffen good: positive. By convention, use absolute value.

📈

Income Elasticity

η_x = (∂x/∂I)·(I/x)

Normal good: η > 0 (demand rises with income).
Inferior good: η < 0 (demand falls with income).

🔄

Cross-Price Elasticity

ε_xy = (∂x/∂p_y)·(p_y/x)

Substitutes: ε_xy > 0 (higher p_y → more x demanded).
Complements: ε_xy < 0 (higher p_y → less x demanded).

Illustration: U=xy → x*=I/2p_x, y*=I/2p_y  |  I=200, p_x=5, p_y=10 → x*=20, y*=10 ε_x = (∂x/∂p_x)·(p_x/x) = (−I/2p_x²)·(p_x/x) = −200/(2·5·20) = −1 η_x = (∂x/∂I)·(I/x) = (1/2p_x)·(I/x) = 200/(2·5·20) = +1 ε_xy = (∂x/∂p_y)·(p_y/x) = 0 · (p_y/x) = 0  (x and y are independent)
