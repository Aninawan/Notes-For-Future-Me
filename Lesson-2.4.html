Consumer Choice and Demand — Lesson Summary & Practice Quiz

01

Marshallian (Ordinary/Uncompensated) Demand

The solution to the utility maximization problem yields the consumer's Marshallian demand functions — demand for each good as a function of all prices and nominal income.

From Max U(x,y) s.t. I = pₓx + p_y·y x* = xm(px, py, I)   —   Marshallian demand for x y* = ym(px, py, I)   —   Marshallian demand for y Example: U = xy  → x* = I / 2px      y* = I / 2py

What it holds constant

Nominal income I and prices of other goods. As px rises, real purchasing power falls.

What varies along the curve

The utility level — as px rises the consumer moves to a lower indifference curve. Both income and substitution effects are at play.

02

Hicksian (Compensated) Demand

The Hicksian demand function holds real income (utility) constant. Income is "compensated" so the consumer stays on the same indifference curve — only the substitution effect is captured.

Hicksian demand function xh = xh(px, py, U)    ← depends on utility U, not income I Identity linking both curves xm[px, py, E(px,py,U)]  =  xh(px, py, U)
Key: The Hicksian demand curve isolates only the substitution effect. It is always downward-sloping (substitution effect is always negative).

04

Indirect Utility & Expenditure Functions

Indirect Utility Function V

Substitute optimal x* and y* into U(·) to get the maximum attainable utility as a function of prices and income.

V = V(p₁, p₂, …, pₙ, I)

Expenditure Function E

Shows the minimum expenditure needed to achieve a given utility level U at prevailing prices.

E = E(p₁, p₂, …, pₙ, U)

Example: U = xy  →  x* = I/2pₓ, y* = I/2p_y V(pₓ, p_y, I) = x* · y* = I² / (4pₓ p_y) V and E are inverse functions of each other V(pₓ, p_y, E) = U₀  ⟺  E(pₓ, p_y, U₀) = I
Shephard's Lemma: ∂E/∂pₓ = xh. A ₱1 rise in pₓ increases minimum required expenditure by exactly xh pesos (the amount of x currently consumed).
05

The Slutsky Equation

Decomposes the total effect of a price change into substitution and income effects.

∂x/∂pₓ
Total Price Effect
Marshallian: how xm changes when pₓ changes (nominal I fixed)
=
∂xh/∂pₓ
Substitution Effect
Hicksian slope; always ≤ 0 (diminishing MRS)
−
x · ∂x/∂I
Income Effect
x times the income slope; sign depends on good type
Slutsky Equation (full form) ∂x/∂pₓ  =  ∂xh/∂pₓ  −  x · (∂x/∂I) Signs by good type (when pₓ rises) Normal:   (−) = (−) − (+)    → both effects reduce x Inferior: (−) = (−) − (−)    → SE dominates, IE partially offsets Giffen:   (+) = (−) − (−)    → IE dominates SE
Key property: The substitution effect ∂xh/∂pₓ is always negative — higher price always reduces compensated demand. Only the income effect can reverse the total response.
06

Deriving Hicksian from Marshallian Demand

1
Start with Marshallian demand: x* = x(pₓ, p_y, I) and y* = y(pₓ, p_y, I)
2
Derive the indirect utility function: Substitute x* and y* into U(x,y) to get V(pₓ, p_y, I) = U₀
3
Solve for I: Invert V to express income as a function of prices and utility → this is the expenditure function E(pₓ, p_y, U₀)
4
Substitute E for I: Replace I in the Marshallian demand with E(pₓ, p_y, U₀). The result is the Hicksian demand xh(pₓ, p_y, U₀).
Example: U=xy → V = I²/4pₓp_y = U₀ → Solve for I I = E = 2√(U₀ · pₓ · p_y) Substitute into x* = I/2pₓ xh = 2√(U₀ pₓ p_y) / 2pₓ = √(U₀ p_y / pₓ)
