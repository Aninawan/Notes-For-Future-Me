Market Demand and Elasticities

1. Individual Demand Curve

The individual demand curve shows the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded, holding other factors constant.

Law of Demand: When the price of a good falls, quantity demanded increases (ceteris paribus).

Demand Shifters

  • Income
  • Prices of other goods
  • Tastes and preferences

Movement along the curve → Caused by price change (change in quantity demanded)

Shift of the curve → Caused by other factors (change in demand)

2. Market Demand

Market demand is the horizontal summation of all individual demand curves.

Market Demand Formula:
X = xA(px, py, IA) + xB(px, py, IB)

All consumers face the same prices and act as price takers.

Movement in market demand → Caused by change in own price.

Shift in market demand → Caused by income, related goods prices, or preferences.

3. Elasticity

Elasticity measures responsiveness of one variable to changes in another.

Own-Price Elasticity of Demand

Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in its own price.

Elasticity Formula:
ε = (% Change in Quantity) / (% Change in Price)

Important Properties:

  • Usually negative (law of demand)
  • We use absolute value to classify magnitude
  • Inelastic: |ε| < 1
  • Unit Elastic: |ε| = 1
  • Elastic: |ε| > 1

4. Price Elasticity and Total Spending

Total Spending = P × Q

If demand is elastic → Price ↑ → Total Spending ↓
If demand is inelastic → Price ↑ → Total Spending ↑
If unit elastic → Total Spending unchanged

Key Rule: The direction of total spending depends on elasticity.

5. Income Elasticity of Demand

η = (% Change in Quantity) / (% Change in Income)
  • Normal Good: η > 0
  • Inferior Good: η < 0

6. Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

θxy = (% Change in Qx) / (% Change in Py)
  • Substitutes: θ > 0
  • Complements: θ < 0

7. Example: Cobb-Douglas Utility (U = xy)

Marshallian Demands:

  • x = I / (2px)
  • y = I / (2py)

Elasticities:

  • Own-price elasticity = −1 (unit elastic)
  • Income elasticity = 1
  • Cross-price elasticity = 0
Cobb-Douglas with equal exponents results in: Unit elastic demand and proportional income response.

Key Takeaways

  • Market demand is the sum of individual demands.
  • Price changes cause movements along curves; other factors cause shifts.
  • Elasticity measures responsiveness.
  • Total spending behavior depends on elasticity.
  • Income and cross-price elasticities classify goods as normal, inferior, substitutes, or complements.
