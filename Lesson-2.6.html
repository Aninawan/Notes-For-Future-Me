Efficiency in Exchange
Among Consumers

Consumer Choice and Demand — Lesson Summary & Practice Quiz

01

Three Pillars of Economic Efficiency

Economic efficiency (≈ Pareto efficiency) requires three conditions to hold simultaneously across the entire economy.

🔄

Exchange Efficiency

Goods X and Y are allocated between consumers so that no reallocation can make someone better off without harming another.

MRS_XY^A = MRS_XY^B
⚙️

Production Efficiency

Labor and capital are used efficiently across industries — no reallocation of inputs can raise one good's output without reducing another's.

MRTS_LK^X = MRTS_LK^Y
⚖️

Product-Mix Efficiency

The rate of substitution in production equals the rate of substitution in consumption — the right mix of goods is produced.

MRT_XY = MRS_XY
This topic covers only Exchange Efficiency. Production and product-mix efficiency are addressed in later topics (Topic 3+).
02

Pareto Improvement & Pareto Optimality

Pareto Improvement

A reallocation of resources that makes at least one person better off without making anyone else worse off. Implies the initial allocation was not efficient.

Pareto Optimal (Efficient)

An allocation where no Pareto improvement is possible — it is impossible to improve one person's welfare without harming another's.

Economic efficiency ≈ Pareto efficiency. A competitive equilibrium achieves Pareto optimality under ideal conditions — this is the First Welfare Theorem.
03

The Exchange Efficiency Condition

Consider two consumers A and B with fixed total supplies of goods X and Y:

Fixed endowments (supply) X̄ = X_A + X_B   |   Ȳ = Y_A + Y_B Utility functions (no consumption externalities) U_A = U_A(X_A, Y_A)     U_B = U_B(X_B, Y_B) Exchange Efficiency Condition MRS_XY^A = MRS_XY^B Recall from utility maximization: MRS = p_X / p_Y At competitive equilibrium, both consumers face the same prices → MRS_A = MRS_B ✓

Why MRS_A ≠ MRS_B implies inefficiency

If A values X relative to Y more than B does, they can trade — A gives Y to B in exchange for X — and both benefit. This is a Pareto improvement, so the initial allocation was not efficient.

What happens through reallocation

As goods are reallocated, each consumer's MRS changes (moving along their indifference curves). The MRS values converge until MRS_A = MRS_B — no further gains from trade.

05

The Edgeworth Box & Contract Curve

Edgeworth–Bowley Box Diagram
P = initial allocation (MRS_A ≠ MRS_B). Q = both better off (Pareto improvement). R = A better off, B unchanged. The contract curve connects all Pareto-optimal allocations.

The "Lens" Region

The area bounded by the two indifference curves passing through P. Any point inside the lens Pareto dominates P — both can be made better off by moving there.

The Contract Curve

The locus of all Pareto-optimal allocations where MRS_A = MRS_B. It connects 0ᴬ to 0ᴮ. All efficient allocations lie on this curve — only distributional choices remain.

Key insight: Starting from any off-curve point like P, trade between A and B will move the allocation into the lens and eventually onto the contract curve. Competitive markets achieve this automatically via the price mechanism.
