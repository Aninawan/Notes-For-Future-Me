ETHICS INTRO

Ethics and Moral Reasoning in Everyday Life

1. What is Ethics?

Ethics comes from the Greek word ethos meaning character. It is the study of the underlying principles of morality.

  • Is there a standard for goodness?
  • What does it mean to be good or bad?
  • Why should we be good?

Unlike GMRC or Values Education, Ethics questions the foundations of morality instead of merely prescribing behavior.

2. Ethics Under Philosophy

Philosophy means “love of wisdom.” It branches into:

  • Logic – proper reasoning
  • Metaphysics – nature of reality
  • Epistemology – study of knowledge
  • Axiology – study of values

Axiology includes:

  • Aesthetics (beauty)
  • Ethics (morality)

Ethics searches for the Summum Bonum (Greatest Good).

3. Three Approaches in Studying Ethics

  • Meta-ethics – studies basic ethical concepts.
  • Normative Ethics – studies ethical theories (How ought I to live?).
  • Applied Ethics – applies ethics to real issues (bioethics, business ethics, environmental ethics, cyber ethics).

4. Terms Used in Ethics

Two uses of “ethical”:

  • Within scope: Ethical vs Non-ethical
  • Evaluative: Ethical (good) vs Unethical (bad)

Good–Bad allows a gradient. Right–Wrong is absolute.

5. Primary Specimen: Morality

Ethics studies morality. Morality developed historically:

  • Folkways – customary practices
  • Mores – socially binding norms
  • Laws – codified moral standards

Mores evolve over time and may differ across cultures. Individuals must reflect on societal standards and their own beliefs.

6. Moral Agents

  • Humans are moral agents because they use reason.
  • Necessary conditions: Freedom and Obligation.
  • Reasoning can be theoretical or practical.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics studies the foundation of morality.
  • It belongs under Philosophy → Axiology.
  • Three approaches: Meta, Normative, Applied.
  • Morality evolved from Folkways → Mores → Laws.
  • Ethics applies to everyday life decisions.
