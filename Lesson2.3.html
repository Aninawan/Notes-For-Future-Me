Income & Substitution Effects

Price Changes, Giffen Goods & Price Consumption Curves

Understanding Price Effects

When the price of a good changes, the consumer's optimal choice shifts. This total change in quantity demanded is composed of two distinct effects: the substitution effect and the income effect.

Total Effect (NE) = Substitution Effect (SE) + Income Effect (IE)

Understanding these components helps explain why quantity demanded changes and reveals consumer behavior patterns.

Substitution Effect

The substitution effect shows how consumers substitute one good for another when its price changes, holding purchasing power constant (staying on the same indifference curve).

Key Characteristics

  • Movement along the same indifference curve: Utility level remains constant
  • MRS must equal new price ratio: Optimal allocation changes even without utility change
  • Relative expensiveness changes: Good becomes relatively cheaper or more expensive

When Price of X Increases

  • px/py increases (X becomes relatively more expensive)
  • Consumer shifts preference toward relatively cheaper good Y
  • Quantity of X demanded decreases: ∂x/∂pxSE < 0
  • Quantity of Y demanded increases: ∂y/∂pxSE > 0

Important Note

Point B (after substitution effect) is not on the original budget constraint. The consumer cannot afford it unless compensated. This is why we must also consider the income effect.

Income Effect

The income effect represents the change in optimal consumption due to a change in purchasing power or real income caused by the price change.

How Real Income Changes

  • When price of X increases: Real purchasing power decreases (cannot buy as much)
  • When price of X decreases: Real purchasing power increases (can buy more)

Effect Depends on Good Type

Normal Good (most common): ∂x/∂RI > 0

When real income decreases due to price increase: quantity demanded decreases further

Inferior Good: ∂x/∂RI < 0

When real income decreases due to price increase: quantity demanded increases (opposite direction)

Movement to Different Indifference Curve

Unlike the substitution effect, the income effect moves the consumer to a completely different indifference curve, reflecting the change in real purchasing power.

Cases: Normal Goods (Both SE and IE in Same Direction)

Case 1: SE = IE (Balanced Effects)

Substitution and income effects are equal in magnitude. Net effect (NE) exists but both goods experience both effects reinforcing one another.

Case 2: SE < IE (Income Effect Dominates)

Income effect is larger than substitution effect. Quantity of X decreases significantly. Consumer experiences substantial loss of purchasing power.

Case 3: SE > IE (Substitution Effect Dominates)

Substitution effect is larger than income effect. Quantity of X decreases, but not as much as when SE = IE. Consumer's substitution behavior dominates income constraint.

For all normal goods: When price increases, both SE and IE lead to decreased quantity demanded, reinforcing the normal downward-sloping demand curve.

Cases: Inferior Goods (Effects in Opposite Directions)

Case 4: SE > IE (Substitution Dominates)

  • Substitution Effect: Quantity of X decreases (becomes relatively expensive)
  • Income Effect: Quantity of X increases (real income falls, inferior good)
  • Net Effect: Quantity of X still decreases overall (SE stronger)

Case 5: SE < IE (Income Effect Dominates) - GIFFEN GOOD

  • Substitution Effect: Quantity of X decreases (becomes relatively expensive)
  • Income Effect: Quantity of X increases (real income falls, inferior good)
  • Net Effect: Quantity of X increases overall (IE stronger) ← VIOLATES LAW OF DEMAND

Giffen Good

A Giffen good is a special case of an inferior good where the income effect is so large that it overwhelms the substitution effect. When price increases, quantity demanded increases—violating the law of demand!

Requirements for Giffen Good

  • Good must be inferior: Demand decreases when income increases
  • Income effect > Substitution effect: In magnitude and opposite direction
  • Good constitutes large budget share: Price change significantly affects real income

Historical Examples

  • Bread in poor Irish households during 19th century
  • Rice in poor Asian households
  • Potatoes during Irish famine

Why Does This Happen?

When the price of a Giffen good (say potatoes) increases, poor consumers' real income drops so much that they can no longer afford more expensive alternatives (meat). They must buy even more potatoes to survive, despite the higher price.

Price Consumption Curve (PCC)

The Price Consumption Curve traces all optimal consumption bundles as the price of one good varies, while income and the price of the other good remain constant.

PCC Derivation

  • Start with initial optimal bundle (point A) at original prices
  • Change the price of good X
  • Find new optimal bundle (point B, then C, etc.)
  • Connect all these optimal points to form the PCC

Relationship to Demand Curve

The Price Consumption Curve can be converted into a demand curve by plotting price (px) against quantity demanded (x).

PCC Slopes and Demand Elasticity

Case 1: Horizontal PCC (Slope = 0)

Unit Elastic Demand (ε = 1)
  • Y quantity doesn't change as price of X changes
  • Total spending on X remains constant: px × x = constant
  • Consumer maintains fixed budget allocation to both goods

Case 2: Upward Sloping PCC (Slope > 0)

Inelastic Demand (ε < 1)
  • Y increases as price of X increases
  • Consumer reduces X, increases Y
  • Budget share of X increases when price increases

Case 3: Downward Sloping PCC (Slope < 0)

Elastic Demand (ε > 1)
  • Y decreases as price of X increases
  • Consumer strongly reduces X, spending shifts to other goods
  • Budget share of X decreases when price increases

Summary: Effects of Price Changes

Normal Goods

When price decreases: Both SE and IE → increase in quantity demanded

When price increases: Both SE and IE → decrease in quantity demanded

Effects reinforce each other, creating clear demand curve

Inferior Goods (Not Giffen)

When price decreases: SE increases quantity, IE decreases it; SE > IE

When price increases: SE decreases quantity, IE increases it; SE > IE

Net effect still follows law of demand (substitution effect dominates)

Giffen Goods

When price increases: IE > SE, so quantity demanded increases

When price decreases: IE > SE, so quantity demanded decreases

Violates law of demand—upward sloping demand curve

Key Takeaways

  • Total effect = Substitution Effect + Income Effect
  • SE: consumer substitutes toward cheaper good, staying on same indifference curve
  • IE: change in real purchasing power moves consumer to different indifference curve
  • For normal goods, both effects reinforce (law of demand holds)
  • For inferior goods, effects oppose; SE typically dominates
  • Giffen goods are rare inferior goods where IE > SE, violating law of demand
  • PCC traces optimal choices as price varies; its slope relates to demand elasticity
  • Understanding these effects explains complex consumer behavior patterns
